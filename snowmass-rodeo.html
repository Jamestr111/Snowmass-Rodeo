What's in the arena

Rodeo action starts at 7:00pm and runs about 90 minutes. Here's what you'll see, event by event.

Opening

Grand Entry

Cowboys and cowgirls ride into the arena carrying flags in tribute to the American Flag — the traditional kickoff to every performance.

Rough Stock

Bareback Riding

Trickier than it looks — with nothing to hold but a suitcase‑like handle on a strap, the rider must stay balanced and coordinated with a powerful horse for a full eight seconds.

Timed Team Event

Team Roping

Partnership, precise timing, and anticipation. The header ropes the steer's horns and turns it left, giving the heeler a shot at its hind legs — rodeo's only true team event.

Rough Stock

Saddle Bronc Riding

Known as "rodeo's classic event." Rooted in breaking saddle horses, it combines strength, style and rhythm — the rider holds a single thick rein and is disqualified for touching the horse or himself with his free hand.

Family Team Event

Dally Ribbon Roping

A roper on horseback catches the calf and dallies the rope, then a runner on foot collects the ribbon from the calf's tail and races it back to the box. Teams must include one male and one female contestant; kids under 13 may enter.

Fan Favorite

Barrel Racing

Three barrels, one horse, one rider, and a cloverleaf pattern to run clean. Timed to the hundredth of a second.

Most Dangerous

Bull Riding

2,000+ pounds of bull, one flat braided rope, one hand free. The most dangerous event in rodeo — and the one with the fewest rules. Eight seconds, no touching yourself or the bull with your free hand.

Added 2023

Breakaway Roping

Blink and you'll miss it. A timed ladies' event and a style of calf roping without tying — the roper throws a lasso around the calf's neck as fast as possible after it breaks from the chute.

For The Little Buckaroos

Kids' Events

Sign‑up happens on the grounds between 5:30pm and 7pm, right before the rodeo starts. A parent or legal guardian must be present to sign a liability release.

Ages 4–10

Calf Scramble

Kids line up in the arena and chase calves wearing ribboned tails, trying to pull a ribbon free. First three to turn one in win awards; everyone who competes gets a prize. No entry limit — sign up before 7pm.

Free · Ages 4–7

Mutton Bustin'

The crowd favorite: little buckaroos hang onto a woolly sheep for eight seconds. Open to kids under 50 lbs. Day‑of entry only — eight contestants are drawn at random from the sign‑up pool.

Scholarships

Every Wednesday night keeps this rodeo running — and helps send its next generation of volunteers and contestants to college.

How It Works

Show up for the season, earn toward your education.

The Snowmass Western Heritage Association has built its scholarship program around the same thing that built the rodeo: showing up, week after week. Volunteers and contestants who work or compete at seven of the season's ten Wednesday night rodeos become eligible for an award toward their education — up to $5,000.

Up to $5,000

Awarded per qualifying student, toward tuition and continuing education.

7 of 10 Rodeos

The season's attendance bar for volunteers and contestants to qualify.

Volunteers & Contestants

Open to the young people who work and compete at the rodeo all summer.

Funded By You

Every ticket, sponsorship, and donation feeds the fund directly.

Where It Leads

Rodeo kids grow up to be everything.

Past recipients have gone on to study law, medicine, environmental science, politics, and veterinary medicine, among other fields. Different paths, same starting point — a summer spent showing up at the arena.

It's a small-town version of a simple idea: the community that raises you can also help send you to school.

Who We Are

Snowmass Western Heritage Association

SWHA is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit whose mission is to inform and educate visitors and area residents about the western heritage and character of Snowmass Village and the Roaring Fork Valley, through events and education.

SWHA produces the Snowmass Rodeo, a summer series every Wednesday evening, June 17 through August 19. 2026 marks our 52nd year. Fundraising supports scholarship opportunities for rodeo students and donations to western‑related organizations in the area.

Our story

52 years of Wednesday nights — the people who started it, the family who runs it now, and the community that shows up.

History

Built in a summer, running for 52 years.

In 1972, a local competitive roper and outfitter named Doug McLain set out to build a weekly rodeo in Snowmass. With about $1,200 and help from friends, he built the arena and bleachers himself. The following summer, roughly 1,400 people showed up for the very first Snowmass Rodeo on June 13, 1973 — before Snowmass Village was even officially an incorporated town.

It sold out then, and it still sells out now. In 2003, a group of Snowmass residents formalized that community effort into a nonprofit — the Snowmass Western Heritage Association — to keep the rodeo, and the western heritage behind it, going for the long run. The rodeo paused for one summer during the COVID-19 pandemic, which pushed its 50th-anniversary season to 2024. 2026 marks the 52nd season.

Still one of the best in the country

Snowmass Rodeo is one of the longest-running rodeos in Colorado, and has repeatedly landed on USA Today's 10 Best Rodeos list nationally.

The People

Who built it, who runs it

A handful of names show up again and again across five decades of Snowmass Rodeo.

Founder

Doug McLain

Built the original arena and bleachers by hand in 1972 and ran the very first rodeo in 1973. Still roping in the arena decades later.

Original Announcer

Twirp Anderson

Co-created the rodeo alongside McLain and called the action from the announcer's booth for 30 years.

Arena Director

Jim Snyder

A longtime local ranch manager, Jim has served as arena director and SWHA board president, keeping the rodeo's western roots intact as ranching itself has modernized.

Executive Director

Darce Vold

A third-generation stock contractor and owner of Triple V Rodeo Company, Darce has produced the Wednesday night rodeo and led SWHA as executive director since 2013.

Founding board, 2003

Archer Bishop, John Canning, Chris Kelly, Doug MacKenzie, Leah Moriarty, Marty Pickett, Edgell Pyles (founding chairman), and Dave Taylor formed SWHA's original board of directors. SWHA remains governed today by a volunteer board of Snowmass Village community members.
